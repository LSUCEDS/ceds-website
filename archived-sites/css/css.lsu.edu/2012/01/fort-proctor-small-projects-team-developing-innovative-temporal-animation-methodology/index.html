Fort Proctor Small Projects Team Developing Innovative Temporal Animation Methodology

January 22, 2012

Fort Proctor is a United States military fort built in 1856 on the edge of Lake Borgne in response to the perceived defensive weakness of New Orleans after the War of 1812. The Fort was never fully realized and construction ceased in 1859 after a large hurricane and the start of the Civil War. The fort has remained a static marker in a highly fluctuating landscape over the course of 150 years. This landscape, a fluid mix of fluvial deposits, slowly shifts through hydraulic processes and has received major intrusions that have altered the relationship between water, edge and land.

The Fort Proctor small projects team is currently working on a series of mapping, observation and surveying tasks to document the past and current conditions of the fort and surrounding site. Surveying and mapping is currently documenting the as built conditions through a series of two site visits. A BIM model is currently being constructed from the original construction documents and this will then be modified to represent the current conditions, creating two models of the site as it was envisioned and of the site as it exists. At the same time students are assembling a larger GIS map that aims to show the major wetland systems, storm paths, vegetation, bathymetry, topography, fluvial processes and climate. The team has identified three scales that will be used to analyze and represent the temporal change that has occurred from 1856 to the present.

The mapping leads to an approach that has focused the team on developing a film that intends to create a temporal experience from identified vantage points throughout the site. Current work is being developed to create this experience as a heuristic design tool, superimposing past and present with overlays of analytical data. Past projects such as Matt Faust’s film “home” and the concept of the flipbook are referenced as case studies towards this end.

The Fort Proctor small projects team recently wrapped up an extensive documentation process of the site and ruins. This documentation has produced detailed technical drawings, precision photography and digital models representing the site and fort as designed (1856), the fort as built (1859) and the current ruins (2011). The site photography for the project has documented the overall fort and many site details. A high resolution panorama was created to document the fort’s southern facade. Full resolution link (warning 35mb).

Combining this vast amount of documentation with extensive research regarding the fort’s construction, historical context and structural testing the team is creating a series of animations. These animations are being developed to consolidate a broad range of temporal data into manageable narratives.

The team is looking at several concepts to integrate broader contextual and specific structural data into illustrative temporal animations.

About the studio

CSS brings together disciplines that often work independently —such as designers, scientists, engineers, and planners—to respond to critical issues of affecting coastal regions.

Through trans-disciplinary design, research, community engagement, and education, the CSS uses innovative approaches to design and foster resilient coastal communities and ecosystems.
