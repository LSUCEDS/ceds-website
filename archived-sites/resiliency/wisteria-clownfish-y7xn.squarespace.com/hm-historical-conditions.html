Hydrological Modeling

 

HISTORICAL CONDITIONS

COMPONENT OF HYDROLOGICAL MODELING

“As urban development expands laterally in the Amite Basin, not only does it affect the behavior of floodwaters, but it also increases the number of people that live within FEMA-designated flood zones.”

 

The greater Baton Rouge region is the primary urban center in the Amite River Basin. However, it is only over the course of the past century that development and urbanization have really taken off here. In the tri-parish area of East Baton Rouge, Livingston, and Ascension Parishes, the total population has exploded more than tenfold from just over 60,000 in 1900 to more than 700,000 in 2020. These three

parishes account for the bulk of the Amite River Basin’s population, however, and population trends in the Basin have not been uniform; three of the four Mississippi counties in the Basin have seen a drop in population since 1900. Six million African-American people left the South during the Great Migration, including from many of the counties and parishes of the Amite Basin, which until then had kept Black labor hostage under the sharecropping system of the Jim Crow era. Urbanization trends and the concentration of population in cities have continued since then, and so has the accompanying expansion of development and impervious surfaces.

Impervious surfaces are surfaces that are not pervious to water—roofs, parking lots, roads, etc. As these surfaces cover more and more ground area, the ability of the soil and vegetation to soak up and slow down water decreases. To analyze the effects of increased impervious cover, Alexandre Cowles (M.S. Coastal and Ecological Engineering) used historical population data and land cover maps to compare flood scenarios based on data representing 1938 and present-day land cover conditions. HEC-HMS and HEC-RAS simulations of various storm events, including historical events like August 2016 as well as hypothetical, spatially uniform design storms, indicate that the expansion of impervious surfaces have led to earlier and higher peak flood flows since 1938. Taking this further, Austin Guerin (M.S. Civil Engineering—Water Resources) has used HEC-FIA to analyze the changes in human risk associated with these historical changes. As urban development expands laterally in the Amite Basin, not only does it affect the behavior of flood waters, but it also increases the number of people that live within FEMA-designated flood zones. Although 70% of Ascension Parish lies within the FEMA 100-year floodplain, for example, its population has still more than doubled since 1983, when the previous record-breaking flood event hit the Amite Basin.

Amite River Basin Commission

To mitigate flood damage in the Amite River Basin, the Amite River Basin Commission serves as a multi-parish authority to accomplish flood control measures; facilitates cooperation between federal, state and local governing bodies to foster floodplain management; maintains and operates structures built under the auspices of the Commission; and coordinates river management within the basin.
